The history of computing is a story of abstraction.
Early programmers worked directly with machine instructions, toggling switches and reading blinking lights.
Then came assembly language, which gave human-readable names to those raw opcodes.
Higher-level languages followed, hiding memory addresses behind variables and loops.
Today a developer can write a handful of lines and invoke capabilities that once required rooms full of hardware.
Each layer of abstraction trades some control for productivity, some visibility for simplicity.
The art of software engineering is knowing when to trust the abstraction and when to peer beneath it.
